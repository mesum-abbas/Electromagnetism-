Section 01

Introduction & Purpose

The Mutual Induction Lab Simulator is an interactive electromagnetic induction tool that models the physical behaviour of two magnetically coupled coils — a primary coil driven by a DC or AC source, and a secondary coil that produces an induced EMF. It visualises all key parameters in real time, allowing students and engineers to explore how changing any physical factor directly impacts induction performance.

The simulator is built on simplified but accurate physics models derived from Faraday's Law of Induction, Neumann's Formula for mutual inductance, and the transformer principle. Every computed value follows real equations, making it a suitable companion for coursework in electromagnetic theory, power systems, and electronics.

Scope

This document covers: all 10 controllable input factors, 8 computed output quantities, 3 source modes, LED output behaviour, physics derivations, experimental findings, real-world applications, and suggested lab exercises.

Section 02

Physics & Theory

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

When the magnetic flux passing through a circuit changes, an electromotive force (EMF) is induced in that circuit. The magnitude of the EMF is proportional to the rate of change of flux.

Faraday's Law · Induced EMF
EMF = −N · (dΦ / dt)
EMFInduced electromotive force (Volts)
NNumber of turns in the coil
ΦMagnetic flux through coil (Weber)
tTime (seconds)
−Lenz's Law: opposes the change

Mutual Inductance — Neumann's Formula

Mutual inductance M quantifies how well a change in current in coil 1 induces a voltage in coil 2. It depends on coil geometry, their separation, and the permeability of the medium between them.

Neumann's Formula (simplified model used in simulator)
M = k · √(L₁ · L₂)
L ∝ μᵣ · N²
MMutual inductance (Henry)
kCoupling factor (0 – 0.99)
L₁, L₂Self-inductance of each coil
μᵣRelative permeability of core
NNumber of turns

Coupling Factor k

The coupling factor is the key composite quantity in the simulator. It represents the fraction of flux from coil 1 that actually links with coil 2. In the simulator, it is computed as:

Coupling Factor Derivation (Simulator Model)
k = k_dist · k_angle · k_shield · (0.5 + 0.5 · k_mu)
k_distDistance factor: 1 − (d−1)/19
k_anglecos(θ) where θ is misalignment angle
k_shield0.3 if shielding ON, else 1.0
k_mulog₁₀(μᵣ+1) / log₁₀(401), range 0–1

Induced EMF on Secondary

Secondary EMF (Simulator)
EMF₂ = M · I₁ · N₂ · 0.06 · (1 + k_mu)
EMF₂Induced EMF on secondary coil (V)
MMutual inductance (mH)
I₁Primary source current (A)
N₂Secondary coil turns

Secondary Circuit

Secondary Current & Power
I₂ = EMF₂ / (R_load + R_wire)
P = I₂² · R_load
I₂Secondary current (A)
R_loadLoad resistance (Ω)
R_wireWire resistance / losses (Ω)
PPower delivered to load (W)
Section 03

Primary Coil Factors

The primary coil is driven by the 9V DC battery and acts as the source of the changing magnetic flux. Three parameters control its behaviour.

N₁ — Primary Turns Count

🔵
N₁ — Primary Turns
Range: 5 – 100 turns
The number of wire windings on the primary coil. Each additional turn adds to the total magnetomotive force (MMF), producing a stronger and larger magnetic flux for the same current.
↑ N₁ → Stronger B field, higher flux Φ, larger M, higher EMF₂
↓ N₁ → Weaker field, lower induced EMF

In the simulator, flux is proportional to I₁ · N₁ · μᵣ. Mutual inductance is proportional to k · √(N₁ · N₂) · μᵣ. Doubling N₁ therefore increases M by approximately √2 ≈ 1.41×.

Key Observations — N₁
N₁ = 5, I = 2A, d = 5cmLow EMF
N₁ = 50, same conditions~3× EMF increase
N₁ = 100, same conditionsMax flux — near saturation

μᵣ — Core Material (Permeability)

The core material controls the magnetic permeability of the medium around which the coil is wound. A ferromagnetic core concentrates magnetic flux and dramatically increases mutual inductance.

Setting Material μᵣ Value Effect
1 Air 1 Baseline — weakest coupling
2 Wood 2 Slight increase in flux
3 Ferrite 10 Common transformer core; significant improvement
4 Iron 100 Strong concentration of flux
5 Silicon Steel 400 Maximum coupling; used in power transformers
Important Note

In the simulator the μᵣ contribution is computed via a logarithmic normalisation: k_mu = log₁₀(μᵣ+1) / log₁₀(401). This means the benefit of upgrading from Air to Ferrite is relatively large, while the jump from Iron to Silicon Steel gives diminishing returns in the coupling factor k.

I — Source Current

The primary source current, controlled by the rheostat slider, ranges from 0.5 A to 10 A. Current is the primary driver of the magnetic flux in the primary coil.

⚡
Source Current I
Range: 0.5 A – 10.0 A (slider 1–20)
Higher current through N₁ turns creates stronger magnetic field intensity H, which drives more flux through the core. Since Φ ∝ N·I·μᵣ, doubling current doubles flux.
↑ I → ↑ Φ, ↑ M, ↑ EMF₂, ↑ I₂, ↑ Power
↓ I → All outputs proportionally reduced
Transformer Principle

In a real transformer, primary current is set by the load on the secondary. In this simulator, the primary current is an independent input, simplifying the model to focus on induction effects.

Section 04

Secondary Coil Factors

The secondary coil receives the induced EMF and drives current through the load. Three parameters control its performance.

N₂ — Secondary Turns Count

The most direct lever for controlling the induced output voltage. By the transformer equation, the secondary EMF is proportional to the turns ratio N₂/N₁.

Transformer Turns Ratio
EMF₂ / EMF₁ ≈ N₂ / N₁
N₂ > N₁Step-up transformer — higher output voltage
N₂ = N₁Isolation transformer — same voltage, isolated
N₂ < N₁Step-down transformer — lower output voltage

R_load — Load Resistance

The external load connected to the secondary coil. It does not affect the induced EMF itself, but directly controls how much current flows and how much power is extracted.

🟢
Load Resistance
Range: 1 Ω – 100 Ω
The resistance of the device being powered. LED, light bulb, or resistor. At low load resistance, more current flows but total power depends on I²R balance.
↑ R_load → ↓ I₂, but ↑ efficiency (less wire loss fraction)
↓ R_load → ↑ I₂, but wire losses become larger fraction
Optimal Loading

Maximum power is transferred when R_load = R_wire (maximum power transfer theorem). At this point efficiency is exactly 50%. For high efficiency, R_load should be much greater than R_wire.

R_wire — Wire Resistance (Losses)

Represents the intrinsic resistance of the coil winding wire. This is the main source of power loss in the secondary circuit and reduces efficiency.

Wire Resistance Effect on Efficiency
R_wire = 0.1 Ω, R_load = 10 Ω~99% efficiency
R_wire = 1 Ω, R_load = 10 Ω~91% efficiency
R_wire = 5 Ω, R_load = 10 Ω~67% efficiency
R_wire = 10 Ω, R_load = 10 Ω~50% (max power transfer)
Section 05

Geometry Factors

Physical positioning and orientation of the two coils relative to each other profoundly affects how much magnetic flux links the secondary. These are the most spatially intuitive factors in the experiment.

d — Coil Separation

Distance between the two coils measured in centimetres. Magnetic field intensity falls with distance, so the flux linkage drops sharply as coils are moved apart.

📏
Coil Separation
Range: 1 cm – 20 cm
In the simulator, the distance factor is modelled as a linear decrease: k_dist = 1 − (d−1)/19. In real life, the falloff is approximately proportional to 1/d³ for coils far from each other.
↑ Distance → ↓ k_dist → ↓ k → ↓ M → ↓ EMF
d = 1 cm → Maximum coupling (k_dist = 1.0)
Real vs. Simulated Falloff

In the simulator, a linear falloff model is used for simplicity. In reality, the flux linkage between two coaxial coils follows a more complex relationship dependent on coil radii and geometry. For closely coupled toroidal transformers, distance is nearly zero and k approaches 0.99.

θ — Coil Alignment Angle

The angle of misalignment between the two coil axes. When both coils are coaxial (0°), maximum flux linkage occurs. As the angle increases toward 90°, the axes become perpendicular and flux linkage drops to zero.

Angle Factor
k_angle = cos(θ)
θ = 0°cos(0°) = 1.0 — perfect alignment
θ = 45°cos(45°) = 0.707 — 30% flux loss
θ = 60°cos(60°) = 0.5 — 50% flux loss
θ = 90°cos(90°) = 0.0 — zero coupling
Angle θ k_angle Flux Linkage % Effect on EMF
0° 1.000 100% Full induction
15° 0.966 96.6% Negligible loss
30° 0.866 86.6% Minor loss
45° 0.707 70.7% Significant reduction
60° 0.500 50.0% Half of maximum
90° 0.000 0% Zero induction
Section 06

Source Modes

Three special modes modify the behaviour of the primary source circuit, each demonstrating a different physical principle.

AC / Continuous Mode

When AC mode is toggled on, the primary source becomes an alternating current supply. This means the magnetic flux is continuously changing (sinusoidally), which induces a continuous EMF in the secondary coil — even without switching the circuit on and off.

This is the fundamental operating principle of all real-world transformers. A DC source only induces EMF at the moment of switching; an AC source induces EMF continuously.

DC vs AC Induction
DC — Switch closed (steady state)EMF = 0 (no flux change)
DC — At moment of switchingBrief EMF pulse
AC — ContinuousContinuous EMF output
Simulator Behaviour

In AC mode, the field line animation frequency increases (multiplied by 3 vs 1.5 in DC), visually representing the higher rate of flux change. The badge shows "LIVE" and all output metrics are active regardless of switch state.

Reverse Polarity

Reverses the direction of current flow in the primary coil. This flips the orientation of the magnetic field (B field) and consequently reverses the direction of the induced EMF in the secondary (Lenz's Law).

In the simulator, this is visually indicated by the wire colour changing from blue (normal) to gold (reversed). The LED blinks in reverse polarity mode to indicate alternating polarity output.

⇄
Reverse Polarity
Toggle: ON / OFF
Flips the sign of the induced EMF. Wire colours turn gold. LED output blinks at 7 Hz. Combined with AC mode, this simulates rapid polarity alternation — key to understanding full-wave rectification.
Effect: EMF magnitude unchanged, direction reversed
LED: Blinks at sin(t·7) > 0 cadence

Magnetic Shielding

A magnetic shield is a material that redirects or absorbs magnetic flux, preventing it from reaching the secondary coil. This is used in sensitive electronics to prevent interference (EMI shielding).

Shielding Effect
k_shield = 0.3 (when shield ON)
k_shield = 1.0 (when shield OFF)
Reduction70% of coupling is blocked when shielding is active
ApplicationMu-metal shielding in real lab equipment

Section 09

Experimental Findings

Based on systematic variation of all input parameters, the following findings summarise the behaviour of the mutual induction system.

Key Findings — Factor Sensitivity

01

Core Material Has the Highest Single-Factor Impact

Switching from Air (μᵣ=1) to Silicon Steel (μᵣ=400) with all other factors constant increases the induced EMF by approximately 4–5×. No other single parameter change produces this magnitude of improvement. This explains why virtually all commercial transformers use ferromagnetic cores.

02

Distance is the Most Rapidly Degrading Factor

Increasing coil separation from 1 cm to 10 cm (half range) reduces the distance factor from 1.0 to ~0.53. In a real solenoid pair, this effect is even more severe (cubic falloff). Keeping coils close together is essential for high coupling.

03

Turns Ratio Controls Output Voltage Independently of Source

With a fixed primary setup, varying N₂ from 5 to 100 scales the output EMF nearly linearly with N₂. This confirms the transformer turns ratio principle: V₂/V₁ ≈ N₂/N₁. Setting N₂ = 2·N₁ doubles the output voltage (step-up configuration).

04

Misalignment at 45° Causes ~30% Output Loss

Since k_angle = cos(θ), a 45° misalignment reduces flux linkage to 70.7% of maximum. At 60° this drops to 50%. This finding demonstrates why real inductive chargers (Qi charging pads) are sensitive to device placement angle.

05

Wire Resistance Disproportionately Affects Efficiency at Low Loads

At R_load = 1 Ω and R_wire = 2 Ω, only 25% of output power reaches the load. At R_load = 100 Ω and R_wire = 2 Ω, over 98% efficiency is achieved. This confirms that impedance matching and minimising winding resistance are critical engineering goals.

06

Combined Factor Optimisation Yields Maximum LED Brightness

Peak LED output (100%) is achieved at: N₁=100, N₂=100, μᵣ=400 (Silicon Steel), I=10A, d=1cm, θ=0°, No Shield, R_load=1Ω, R_wire=0.1Ω. This represents ideal transformer conditions. Any single factor degradation noticeably reduces the LED output.

07

Shielding Reduces Coupling by 70%

Enabling magnetic shielding forces k_shield = 0.3, meaning only 30% of the normal coupling remains. This is a strong demonstration of EMI shielding principles used in sensitive electronics, MRI machines, and military avionics.

Section 10

Practical Outreach

The simulator bridges abstract electromagnetic theory with hands-on understanding. Here is guidance for practical use across different educational contexts.

For Physics / Electrical Engineering Courses

Use the simulator as a companion to:

1

Verify Faraday's Law Qualitatively

Turn on AC mode and vary the primary current slider. Observe that higher rate-of-change (higher current magnitude) increases the induced EMF — directly demonstrating that EMF ∝ dΦ/dt.

2

Investigate the Transformer Equation

Keep N₁ constant and vary N₂ from 5 to 100. Record the EMF output at each step. Plot EMF vs N₂. The result should be linear, confirming V₂ ∝ N₂.

3

Demonstrate the Role of Core Material

Cycle through all 5 core materials with all other parameters fixed. Record M and EMF at each step. This clearly shows why transformer manufacturers use silicon steel rather than air cores.

4

Explore Maximum Power Transfer

Fix all other parameters. Vary R_load from 1 to 100 Ω and record output power. Plot P vs R_load. Identify the load where power is maximised (when R_load ≈ R_wire).

5

Model Wireless Charging (Qi)

Set d = 2 cm, θ = 0°, AC mode, medium turns, ferrite core. This approximates a wireless phone charging pad. Then increase θ to simulate the phone being placed off-centre — watch the coupling and EMF drop.

Section 11

Real-World Applications

The mutual induction principle demonstrated in this simulator underlies a vast range of modern technologies.

🔌
Power Transformers
AC power grids step voltage up for transmission (reducing I²R losses) and step down for household use. Silicon steel cores, thousands of turns, k ≈ 0.98–0.99.
📱
Wireless Charging (Qi)
Two tightly coupled coils at 1–5 mm distance. Ferrite shields prevent radiation into the device. Operates at 100–350 kHz. Misalignment sensitivity demonstrable directly in simulator.
🏥
MRI Machines
Radiofrequency coils induce signals in body tissue. Superconducting primaries create enormous B fields. Mu-metal shielding (like simulator shield mode) isolates the patient volume.
📡
RFID & NFC
Passive RFID tags have no battery. The reader coil induces enough EMF (via tight coupling at very close range) to power the tag's chip and transmit a response. Exactly this simulator's model.
🚗
EV Inductive Charging
Floor-mounted primary coil charges a car's battery via a secondary coil in the vehicle floor. Operating at 85 kHz, k ≈ 0.7–0.9. Distance and alignment are critical — matching the simulator parameters exactly.
⚙️
Induction Motors
The stator acts as the primary, inducing currents in the squirrel-cage rotor (secondary). The interaction of induced currents and the rotating field produces torque. Mutual inductance drives the entire machine.

Faraday's Law — Historical Context

Historical Milestone — 1831

Michael Faraday (1791–1867) was a British experimental physicist who discovered electromagnetic induction on August 29, 1831. By wrapping two coils of wire around an iron ring and connecting one to a battery and the other to a galvanometer, he observed that the galvanometer needle deflected only at the moments of making or breaking the battery circuit — exactly DC switching induction as simulated here.

Faraday's quantitative law was later formalised by James Clerk Maxwell as one of the four Maxwell's Equations:

Maxwell–Faraday Equation (Integral Form)
∮ E · dl = − d/dt ∫∫ B · dA
EElectric field (V/m)
BMagnetic flux density (Tesla)
∮Line integral around closed loop
∫∫Surface integral through the loop area

Faraday also discovered Lenz's Law (the negative sign in Faraday's law): the induced current always flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is why the LED blinks when polarity is reversed — the secondary opposes the reversal.

Section 14

Simulator Limitations & Notes

Simplified Physics Model

The simulator uses a dimensionally consistent but simplified model. Several real-world effects are not modelled: hysteresis losses, eddy current losses, skin effect, self-capacitance of coils, saturation of ferromagnetic cores, temperature effects on resistance, and near-field radiation.

Aspect Simulator Model Real-World Behaviour
Distance falloff Linear (1 − d/19) Approximately 1/d³ for point sources
Core permeability Log-normalised Nonlinear, temperature-dependent, hysteretic
EMF magnitude Proportional model (× 0.06) Requires coil geometry, dI/dt, exact flux
Efficiency cap Hard cap at 99% Modern power transformers reach 98–99.5%
AC frequency Visual only (animation) 50/60 Hz; frequency affects losses significantly
Load interaction No back-EMF from load Heavy loading reduces primary flux (real transformer)

Glossary of Terms

Term Symbol Definition
EMF ε, V Electromotive Force — the voltage induced by a changing magnetic flux. Measured in Volts.
Magnetic Flux Φ The total magnetic field B passing through a surface. Measured in Webers (Wb).
Flux Density B Magnetic flux per unit area. B = Φ/A. Measured in Tesla (T).
Mutual Inductance M A quantity describing how efficiently one coil induces EMF in another. Measured in Henrys (H).
Coupling Factor k Dimensionless ratio (0–1) indicating what fraction of flux from the primary links with the secondary.
Permeability μᵣ Ratio of a material's magnetic permeability to that of free space. Air = 1, Silicon Steel ≈ 400.
Turns Ratio n Ratio N₂/N₁. Determines step-up (n>1), isolation (n=1), or step-down (n<1) transformer function.
Lenz's Law − The induced current opposes the change in flux that caused it. Accounts for the negative sign in Faraday's Law.
Faraday's Law ε=−NdΦ/dt Induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux linkage with the circuit.
Solenoid — A coil of wire wound around a cylindrical core. Forms the basis of the primary and secondary coils.
Rheostat — A variable resistor used to control the current through the primary coil. Modelled by the current slider.
MMF F = N·I Magnetomotive Force — the "driving force" for magnetic flux. Product of current and turns.
Efficiency η Ratio of output power to input power, expressed as a percentage. Losses reduce η below 100%.
